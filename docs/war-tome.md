# THE WAR-TOME

***Klerg-Tome***

**Lore Reference for the Bloodrock Hai**

**ISE HAI! — NOOOGRAH!**

*Living document. Updated continuously.*

*Companion volumes: The Bloodrock Tome · The Urucku*

---

## 1. About This Tome

> *"Sieh da klerg-tome. Gruk da hai." — Read the war-tome. Know the hai.*

The War-Tome is the third of the great Bloodrock volumes. Where *The Bloodrock Tome* preserves the language and history of the clan, and *The Urucku* sets down the virtues by which the orcs of Bloodrock live and kill, the War-Tome is concerned with what surrounds the line: the fort the hai defends, the units who hold it, the gods invoked above it, and the umies who come against it.

This is a reference. It is meant to be opened to a page, read for what is needed, and closed again. Every entry is written in tome voice — the in-world voice of the hai itself — and is intended to serve any orc, scribe, or outsider who must speak rightly of Bloodrock's war and faith.

**Companion volumes.** *The Bloodrock Tome* (language, lore, the clan's history) and *The Urucku* (the Three Principles and Nine Virtues, the bloodlines of the warrior orders) are the canonical sources. The War-Tome stands beside them, not above them. Where they speak, they are right.

---

## 2. The Three Principles

> *Da Duhkta — the three foundations of orcish virtue.*

All orcish virtue flows from three fundamental principles, known as the *Duhkta*. They are the bones beneath the nine virtues of the Urucku, and every orc — from the meanest snotling to the holder of the stone — lives by them whether he knows their names or not.

**Clog — Might Makes Right.** The first and most fundamental of the Duhkta. The strong take what they want; the weak serve or die. There is no court of appeal. When orcs disagree, they settle it with fists and axes. The winner is right because the winner is strongest. Even the beasts of the field know this; only the umies have invented courts and laws to pretend otherwise.

**Faugh — Battle Cleverness.** The second principle, the cunning of the warrior. Brute strength alone does not win every fight. Faugh is reading the field, striking at the right moment, knowing when to charge and when to fall back. It is speed of body and speed of mind both. The orc who thinks faster than his enemy will land the killing blow.

**Snarf — Deception.** The third principle: lies and trickery as weapons no less worthy than steel. Snarf teaches that the umies' notions of "fair play" are not for orcs. Ambushes, false retreats, poisoned blades, honeyed words that lead enemies into traps — all are Snarf. The orc who tricks a umie into walking off a cliff has won as surely as the orc who pushes him.

From the Duhkta arise the nine virtues of the Urucku. To learn them more fully, see *The Urucku*, the second of the great Bloodrock volumes.

## 3. The Fort

> *"Geep R'heez! Shu'uk Choks! an Gukkda Uld Bludchok'hai Fort!"*

The fort is the heart of the hai. It is the place the chant rises from, the place the wargs are kennelled, the place the bone-pile grows tall. It is named at the founding and remembered by that name long after its walls have fallen.

A Bloodrock fort is not a clean thing. It is built crooked, of chok and r'hee and whatever the peons could drag from the wood that season. The walls bear the scars of every klerg they have weathered — the scorch where a umie rite burned through, the stones laid in by a Klerggoth two successions dead, the hut rebuilt three times because the hai will not let it stand empty. Long-held walls earn names; walls that fall once and are raised again are watched warily by those who hold them.

Within the wall sit the great houses of the hai: the Klerggoth's hall, the peon pit, the warrior huts of every order, the Mojoka huts kept apart from the rest, the forge of the Gukka, the bone-pile where the dead of the klerg are gathered. Beyond the wall, in caves and high places, sit the temples of the Tra. The fort is the heart, but the heart is not the whole of the body.

To lose the fort is a great sorrow. To raise a new one elsewhere is a great labor. Both have happened in the history of Bloodrock, more than once. The hai endures the loss of walls; what it cannot endure is the loss of the chant.

## 4. The Units of the Hai

> *Each orc has a place. The hai endures because the place is kept.*

The orcs of Bloodrock are not interchangeable. Each kind of orc holds a place in the hai — earned, inherited, or thrust upon them — and each carries its own pride, prejudice, and purpose. What follows is the canonical roster: eleven core kinds of orcs who make the hai what it is, and two further kinds reserved for forts large enough, old enough, or fortunate enough to hold them.

These entries are written in tome voice. They are the source of truth for who each orc is — personality, social standing, role in the fort — and they should be read by any scribe, player, or builder who must speak rightly of the units of the hai.

### Core Units

**Snotling** — *The runts. The shunned. The future, if they survive.*

Snotlings are orcs born small — undersized at birth, slow to grow, the ones whose shoulders never broaden and whose voices never deepen. In a hai that measures worth by stature, they are mocked, kicked, and given the work no other orc will do. But snotlings are not nothing. They are quick, they are clever in the cunning way of small things, and they remember every cruelty done to them. Many a Kaptain has fallen to a snotling's blade in the dark. They swarm the tunnels, run messages between the units, and crawl into spaces no Grunt could fit. To survive snotlinghood is to be marked — for life, for grudge, for greatness.

**Peon** — *The whipped backbone of the hai.*

Peons are the laborers — the ones who mine the chok, fell the r'hee, drag the corpses to the bone-pile, and rebuild the wall after every klerg. They bear no weapons of war and earn no bludgelt. They sleep in the peon pit and eat what is left. To call an orc "peon" is half-insult, half-fact: most peons are former snotlings who never grew strong enough for the line, or Grunts broken in battle and reassigned. Yet without peons, no fort stands. The Klerggoth who forgets this loses his walls to crumbling stone before any umie ever reaches the gate.

**Gukka** — *The makers. The hands that build what the line breaks.*

Gukka are the crafters of the hai — the orcs whose hands shape what the rest of the clan shatters. They forge the ggrultuks and the mougs, hammer ring armor for the line, stitch lir'tyk into hide and harness, raise the wall after every klerg, and tend the bone-fires when no one else will. The word itself comes from *gukka* — to create — and the orc named for it bears the title with quiet pride. A skilled Gukka is worth more to the fort than three Brutes; lose your Gukka and you lose your wall, then your weapons, then within a season your hai itself. They do not stand in the line, but the wisest Klerggoth keeps his Gukka safe behind the wall and well-fed at the fire.

**Grunt** — *The clompers. The horde. The line.*

The Grunt is the standard orc warrior — full-grown, weapon-trained, bloodied, and proud of it. When the chant rises and the gate swings open, it is the Grunts who pour through, axes and clubs and spears in hand, screaming **"Nooograh!"** into the faces of the umies. Most orcs in Bloodrock live and die as Grunts, and there is no shame in this. To be a Grunt is to belong fully — to march in the line, to charge in the wedge, to share the fless and the cha at the fire pit afterward. A Klerggoth's true measure is not the count of his Kaptains but the loyalty of his Grunts.

**Skowt** — *Eyes of the hai. The patient kill from cover.*

Skowts are the scout-archers of the hai — orcs whose patience and eye outweigh their bulk. They are lean and restless, too solitary for the wall and too dangerous to keep on it. They range ahead of the fort, watching the tree line for the glint of umie helms; they know every game trail, every old fence post, every place a umie patrol might bed down for the night. Their cry of **"Umies! Klerg jat!"** is the sound that wakes the fort.

In battle, the Skowt waits. From the parapet, from the tree line, from the shadow of a half-built wall, the Skowt looses *lursk'a* after *lursk'a* into the umie line and watches them fall. A skilled Skowt counts his kills not in heads taken but in the moments of confusion he creates — the captain dropped before he could give the order, the standard-bearer down before the charge was joined. When the gate is breached, the Skowts in the towers do not run; they fire down into the press until they are out of arrows or until the hai is saved.

**Brute** — *The wall of meat. The breaker of lines.*

Brutes are the orcs who grew. Where most stand at six feet, the Brute towers seven or more, broad as a wargcart, slow to anger but unstoppable once roused. They fight heavy and they fight low — a one-handed club, axe, or iron-shod mougg paired with the great shield that is the mark of their kind. A single Brute is a force; a wedge of Brutes locks shields and advances as one grinding wall, the kind of formation that crumples a umie charge before it has properly begun. They are not stupid, despite what their enemies say — they are deliberate, taking each fight as if it were the only one.

The greatest among them, after enough klergs and enough skulls, are given shields cut from the bones of beasts and umies alike. These are the **Bone Wall** — the unbreakable rank, the hai's iron edge, an honor older than most living Klerggoths. Where the Bone Wall stands, the gate holds.

**Howl'r** — *The animal-tamer. The keeper of teeth that are not his own.*

Howl'rs are the beast-handlers of the hai. They are the only orcs who hunt the wargs in the deep wood, drag them back snarling in chains, and break them to harness — and from the Howl'rs alone come every warg the hai rides. A Klerggoth who wants mounts for his Skowts asks the Howl'rs. A Kaptain who wants a warg-pack at his flank asks the Howl'rs. None ride without their leave.

Their craft does not end at wolf-flesh. The greatest Howl'rs have brought to heel things no umie dares look upon — giant spiders dragged from the deep wood, blood apes prised from the mountain caves, slugs the size of warcarts, and worse beasts whose names are kept among the Howl'rs alone. A Howl'r speaks in growls and clicks and the rough chanting tongue the beasts answer to. They share their meat with their charges and sleep among them. To wake a Howl'r in the night is to risk the teeth of whatever pack he has bedded down with that season.

**Kaptain** — *Lord. Squad-master. The Klerggoth's iron.*

A Kaptain is an orc who has earned the right to lead other orcs. There may be many Kaptains in a single fort — one for each section of the wall, one for each warband, one for the peons, one for the Skowts. They are confirmed by the Klerggoth, by clomp, or by the consent of those they lead, depending on the era. A Kaptain is not an administrator — a Kaptain is a Grunt who outfought every other Grunt around him, and now bears the weight of telling them when to charge, when to hold, and when to fall back. The word serves also as honorific: *Kaptain Krothu*, in formal address.

**Klerggoth** — *Warlord. Chieftain. The one who holds the stone.*

The Klerggoth is the warlord of the entire hai — there is only one, and the one is absolute. The title is taken, not given. Some Klerggoths inherit by clean succession; many seize the rank in *klomp*, the formal challenge-fight; some, like Bashnak in the old days, win it by the poisoned blade in the dark. The Klerggoth holds the guildstone, names the fort, declares the wars, and answers for every orc who falls in them. The greatest are remembered by name — Grunk, Trugagh, Kyth, Vrishnak, Krothu, Bargtohk, Zut'lug. The lesser are forgotten by morning. To be Klerggoth is to *be* the fort.

**Wierdling** — *Touched by mojo. Untrained, unsteady, useful.*

Wierdlings are orcs born with the stink of mojo on them but without the discipline to wield it cleanly. They see what is not there. They speak to things that may or may not answer. Their hair grows wrong; their eyes catch the firelight strangely. A Wierdling can sometimes call a small mojo — a flicker of flame, a moment of luck, a umie's bowstring snapping at the wrong moment — but cannot explain how, and cannot do it twice the same way.

The hai keeps its Wierdlings at a careful distance. They are scorned and used as snotlings are scorned and used, given the work no Grunt would deign to do, mocked at the fire pit, struck for impertinence. Yet no orc is ever quite easy around one. There is the suspicion, always, that a Wierdling slighted today may be a Mojoka feared tomorrow — and the gods, it is whispered, remember slights done to those they have touched. Most Wierdlings do not survive the long apprenticeship of cruelty. The ones who do are no longer quite Wierdlings.

**Mojoka** — *Wielder of mojo. Tool of the Tra.*

Mojokas are the disciplined mages of Bloodrock — a small, strange, and enigmatic order within the hai, feared and revered in equal measure. They are intelligent and cruel, frail of body and terrible of power. Where a Wierdling stumbles into mojo, a Mojoka commands it — drawing on mojo plak, on the prayers of Krull'nuk, and on the long, painful schooling that began in their Wierdling days and never truly ends.

The hai's old superstition guards them. To strike down a Mojoka without cause is to draw the gaze of the Tra themselves, and the saying *"nub klomp mojoka"* shields them even from Grunts who might otherwise strike them down for being odd.

Mojokas are not many, and the hai prefers them few. Bloodrock is martial first; an overabundance of mages would soften the clan into something unrecognizable, and the Tra do not love a soft hai. A Klerggoth who has even one true Mojoka in his fort counts himself fortunate.

### Optional Units

*Reserved for forts that earn them. A fresh hai begins with the eleven core units; Reavers and Elders unlock through campaign progression, geography (a coastal fort), or sustained survival across multiple Klerggoth lifetimes.*

**Reaver** — *The sea-dog. The bringer of bludgelt across the salt.*

Reavers are the sea-going raiders of the hai — orcs whose lust for bludgelt cannot be sated by the raid on land. They sail the orc longships out of Smugglers Den and the rough coves of the borderlands, fall upon umie merchant ships, sack coastal villages, and return heavy with *rubug*, *kushma*, and prisoners. The sea is a cruel teacher; a Reaver who lives long enough is half-pirate, all blade. They are crafty as well as brutal — martial orcs with the cunning to read tides and weather — and they hold themselves apart from the land-bound Grunts with a quiet pride. Where the ocean ends, so ends a Reaver's interest.

**Elder** — *The voice of old klergs. The keeper of the chant.*

Elders are the orcs who have outlived their warlord years — former Klerggoths, former Kaptains, former Howl'rs whose throats can no longer carry the howl. They have earned the right to sit by the fire and be heard. An Elder remembers the names of every fort the hai has held, every Klerggoth who held it, every wall that fell and every wall that did not. They lead rituals, settle disputes no Kaptain wishes to settle, and teach the snotlings the chant. Xegugg, the first female Elder, is canon. Elders cannot be killed without consequence — even the most reckless Reaver knows this.

## 5. The Tra

> *Kwat da Tra! — the three of three, all gods named at once.*

The Tra are the three gods of Bloodrock — Krull'nuk, Krenbluk, and Belok. They are not loved as the umies are said to love their soft gods. They are feared, fed, and shouted at. The chant **"Kwat da Tra!"** rises from the unruly rabble of any Bloodrock fort on any night the ale is good and the chok is high — three of three, the full count, all gods named at once because no orc wants to leave a god out and find that god remembering it later.

Most orcs hold their faith plainly and without nuance: the Tra are real, the Tra are dangerous, the Tra do not need to be loved but must not be slighted. The deeper questions are left to the Mojoka, who are the avatars of the gods within the hai and whose temples keep the rites the rank-and-file would not know how to perform. To respect a Mojoka is to respect her god. To strike one without cause is to draw the gaze of the Tra themselves.

### Krull'nuk — The Green Trickster

Krull'nuk wears the green of moss and venom. He is the god of mojo, of poison, of all the things the umies would call sorcery. He is also a trickster: the orc who calls Krull'nuk for help may receive it, but rarely in the form expected, and not always to the orc's benefit. His Mojoka are the most numerous of the orders, for mojo is his domain and most of the hai's magic flows through them. He is fed with explosives, with poisons, and with the residue of mojo well-cast.

### Krenbluk — The Red Mother

Krenbluk is the mother goddess of the orcs. She wears red, and her sacrament is blood. The hai itself is said to come from her — she gave birth to Bloodrock in the dark before the first fort was raised. Where the line bleeds, Krenbluk watches; where the chok is wet with the blood of an enemy, she has been honored without ceremony. She is fed with horse flesh and horse fetuses — the meat of a beast that umies prize and orcs do not, given freely back to her.

### Belok — The Black Taker

Belok is the god the umies, if they understood, would call a devil. He wears the black of dried blood and old bone. He is the taker of the dead, the one who walks the battlefield after the last sword has fallen, claiming what is his. His domain is necromancy, and the Mojoka of his order alone hold the keys to that art — no orc speaks to or raises the dead without first consulting a Belok-Mojoka. He is fed with corpses and bones; the bone-pile of every fort is, in its way, an offering to him.

### Worship in the Hai

Most orcs of the hai keep their faith simple: they fear all three gods, they shout **"Kwat da Tra!"** before charging, and they leave the precise theology to those who have time for such things. Temples to each Tra are kept in different places — some near the fort, some far afield in caves and high places — each tended by Mojoka of that god's order. A young Mojoka does not join a temple until she is called; the call comes when it comes, and not before.

The gods themselves are not easily summoned. They will not answer most prayers. Worship is not what the Tra demand from their orcs — what they demand is respect for their avatars, the Mojoka. An orc who slights a Mojoka has, in the eyes of the hai, slighted the Tra she serves. The hai responds accordingly.

## 6. The Umies

> *"Gruk da Urucku. Klomp da umies."*

Umies are the soft, two-legged creatures who came to the world later than the orcs and have been trying to flatter themselves into mastery of it ever since. They live in towns, build with cut stone and clean mortar, and worship gods who ask politeness of them. They tell themselves stories of honor, of compassion, of justice, and they go to war believing those stories matter. They are dangerous because they are many and well-fed; they are weak because they cannot bring themselves to do what an orc would do without thinking twice.

The umies follow eight virtues, enshrined in their temples and recited in their courts. Bloodrock follows the nine of the Urucku, which are the truer, harder virtues those eight are pretending not to be. Where the umies bow to Honor, the orcs hold to Ogba and break the umie's shield-wall. Where the umies whisper of Compassion, the orcs feed the bone-pile and Belok smiles. Each umie virtue is the soft echo of a real orcish one; for the full reckoning, see *The Urucku*, "The Corruption of Human Shrines."

### Their Orders

The umies muster for war in orders, each tied to one of their virtues. They are easy to tell apart at a distance: each order wears its colors openly and behaves true to the virtue it claims. The Order of Honor charges the front gate, refusing traps, scorning ambush. The Rangers of Justice fight in the open field with rank-and-file archery, expecting the orcs to fight by the same rules. The Paladins of Compassion will not leave their wounded. The Mage Tower advances slowly, in long rites that take time to complete. The Knights of Valor charge until they die. The Inquisitors of Honesty have eyes that pierce orcish deceptions and tongues that promise mercy they cannot grant. The Disciples of Sacrifice spend their own bodies to wound the hai. The Monks of Humility wear no armor and ask for none.

Each order has been bled by the hai before. Each will come again. Their virtues are also their weaknesses, for an orc who knows his Urucku knows where to strike.

## 7. Resources of the Hai

> *Chok, r'hee, fless, and bludgelt — the four pillars of any hai.*

The hai is fed and built and armored by what its orcs can drag, hew, hunt, or take. The chief resources of any Bloodrock fort are listed below in their orcish names. For fuller definitions, see the *Lexicon of the Hai* at the end of this volume.

| Resource | Orcish | Source |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Stone | Chok | Mined by peons. Walls, towers, shrines. |
| Wood | R'hee | Felled from the wood. Palisade, scaffolding, fire. |
| Meat | Fless | Hunted or looted. Feeds the hai. |
| Loot | Bludgelt | Taken from umie corpses and raids. |
| Reagents | Mojo plak | Gathered, raided, or grown. Feeds Mojoka rites. |
| Hides | Lir'tyk | Skinned from beasts and umies. Light armor, harness. |
| Bones | K'nabb'h | From the dead. Bone-pile, totems, the Bone Wall. |
| Ale | Krenbluk'a cha | Brewed from grain. Morale, ritual, occasional disaster. |

Of these, *bludgelt* is the truest measure of a hai's strength. A fort without bludgelt is a fort without raids, and a fort without raids will not feed itself for long. Bloodrock has never been a hai of farmers, and the hai that becomes one has stopped being Bloodrock.

## 8. Historical Klergs

> *The names of the great wars, told briefly and in order.*

The hai has known many klergs in its long life. The fullest record sits in *The Bloodrock Tome*, in the chapter on the history of the clan. What follows is a brief reckoning of the great wars and turning points by which the hai measures its own age, given here so that any orc opening the War-Tome may know where in the long story he stands.

### The Founding (1998)

Bloodrock began with a lone orc named Grunk and a thatched-roof cottage east of Cove. The first lieutenants — Gompf, K'Chomp, Trugagh — stood beside him. The hai was small. The klergs were small. The hai built its first wall, raised its first guildstone, and learned the first hard lessons of a fort in hostile country.

### The First Fall (1998)

The town of Oasis turned hostile. Members defected. Klerggoth Grunk vanished without word, and within a single moon the hai had halved. Trugagh tried to hold the stone, then disbanded what remained. The hai slept for a season. It did not die.

### The Rebirth (1999) — Great Lakes

Trugagh returned, raised a new fort east of Cove on a different shore, and called the hai back to it. The hai answered. Bloodrock rose again with a smaller, harder cast, and the wall was rebuilt for the second time.

### The Kyth Era (2000) — The 216 Wars

Klerggoth Kyth took the stone from his brother Trugagh and turned the hai outward. Every war declared on Bloodrock was accepted. By June of 2000 the hai stood at the head of all the wars in the world — two hundred and sixteen at the peak, a number unmatched before or since. The fort never fell. The hai wore ring armor, mounted nothing, and held a fort it could not ban its enemies from. It is the klerg every later Klerggoth measures himself against.

### The Succession Wars (2001–2003)

Kyth's reign ended in his own time. Vrishnak followed, the first Klerggoth named by vote of the hai. He was killed in single combat by the assassin Bashnak, who took the stone with poison — the first Klerggoth to win the rank that way. Xegugg followed Bashnak, the first female Klerggoth, and later the first female Elder. The hai learned in these years that the stone could change hands many ways.

### The Age of Shadows (2003)

The world changed under the hai's feet. Many old orcs walked away. The hai went into a long quiet. Trugagh returned again to hold the stone steady, and Thigog the bowyer was crowned afterward in a klomp the hai still tells stories about.

### Angel Island and Second Age (2007–2010)

Grultuk and Ne'rull moved the hai to a player-run shore called Angel Island. The hai built a fort, fought for it, and held it. When the shore became inhospitable the hai moved again, this time to Second Age, where Kraarug led it to dominance.

### Krothuland (2011)

Klerggoth Krothu turned the hai outward as Kyth had done a decade before — but harder, and farther. The hai marched on the umie province of Cove and erased it. The territory was renamed Krothuland for as long as the hai held it. Krothu is remembered as one of the great Klerggoths.

### UO Renaissance (2014–2017)

LudKrud led the hai fortless for a season, then established an outpost in the Desert of Compassion. Old names returned to stand beside him — Krothu, Vishakt, Bogugh, Zorglug. Xegugg came back also, carrying the warhammer she had carried since the old days, and called the peons to mine and the gukka to raise the wall. Bloodrock had a fort again.

### Smugglers Den (2018–Present)

The hai moved to UO Outlands and chose a place called Smugglers Den — a cave, a fire pit, fallen logs, a defensible rock bridge. Klerggoth Bargtohk raised the fort, sailed warships against the umies of Nusero, and brought home enough bludgelt to make the new fort strong. The current Klerggoth is Zut'lug, who holds the stone as of this writing.
